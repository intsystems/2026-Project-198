\begin{abstract}
  Modern vision models excel at recognition but fail to grasp geometric relationships like symmetry composition. This work investigates whether neural networks can internalize the algebraic 
  structure of the dihedral group $D_4$ (square symmetries) rather than memorizing visual patterns. Using a Siamese $D_4$-equivariant encoder and embedding similarity scorings, we 
  train a model to predict whether two images are related by a $D_4$ transformation 
  and identify the specific element. Here, \emph{Siamese} refers to a dual-branch 
  weight-sharing architecture that processes image pairs jointly, and 
  \emph{equivariant} means the encoder's embeddings transform predictably under 
  the same group action applied to the input. We demonstrate that the model learns true group properties: invariance to operation sequences 
  ($\mathtt{horizontal\_flip} \to \mathtt{vertical\_flip} \equiv \mathtt{rotate\_180}$), canonical element representation, and correct rejection of unrelated pairs. Analysis of attention 
  maps and embeddings reveals internal encoding of the  multiplication table. Unlike VLMs that fail at such tasks, our architecture captures symbolic geometric structures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

% --- (1) Research goal and motivations ---
Deep learning has achieved remarkable success in visual recognition tasks~\cite{he2016deep, dosovitskiy2021image}, yet recent evaluations reveal that even state-of-the-art vision-language models struggle with spatial reasoning: they fail to reliably judge relative positions, estimate distances, or identify geometric relationships between objects in a scene~\cite{chen2024spatialvlm, cheng2024spatialrgpt}. A particularly underexplored failure mode is \emph{compositional} geometric reasoning --- for instance, recognizing that applying a horizontal flip followed by a vertical flip is equivalent to a $180°$ rotation. The goal of this work is to investigate whether a neural network can internalize the algebraic structure of a symmetry group --- not merely memorize visual patterns --- and to identify the architectural choices that make this possible.

% --- (2) Object of research, main termini ---
As a testbed we use the dihedral group $D_4$, the symmetry group of the square. $D_4$ is a non-abelian group of order~8 comprising four rotations ($e, r_{90}, r_{180}, r_{270}$) and 
four reflections ($\sigma_h, \sigma_v, \sigma_{d}, \sigma_{d'}$), equipped with a well-defined multiplication table. Its non-commutativity and subgroup hierarchy make it the smallest group 
that is algebraically nontrivial while remaining small enough for exhaustive behavioral analysis of model predictions.

% --- (3) The problem / challenge ---
We formalize the task as follows: given a pair of images $(I_1, I_2)$, the model must determine whether $I_2$ can be obtained from $I_1$ by applying some element $g \in D_4$, and if so, identify~$g$. This is harder than standard image classification because the model must implicitly learn that composition of operations is well-defined, that every transformation has an inverse, and that visually distinct operation sequences may correspond to the same group element. A further challenge is the \emph{rejection} case: the model must correctly identify pairs not related by any element of~$D_4$.

% --- (4) Methodology: literature review and state-of-the-art ---
% --- (7) Pros and cons of recent works ---
Several lines of prior work inform our approach. \emph{Equivariant architectures.} Cohen and Welling~\cite{cohen2016group} introduced Group Equivariant CNNs (G-CNNs), showing that encoding symmetries into network layers improves sample efficiency. Weiler and Cesa~\cite{weiler2019general} generalized this to $E(2)$-equivariant steerable CNNs, providing a flexible library (\texttt{e2cnn}) for building networks equivariant to arbitrary subgroups of the Euclidean group. However, these works focus on building equivariance \emph{into} the feature extraction pipeline; they do not address whether the network can discover the \emph{group operation itself} from data. \emph{Algebraic reasoning in neural networks.} Power et al.~\cite{power2022grokking} demonstrated that small transformers can learn binary operations on finite groups through a phenomenon called ``grokking'': generalization occurs long after overfitting. Nanda et al.~\cite{nanda2023progress} provided mechanistic interpretability for this, showing that the networks discover Fourier-based representations of modular arithmetic. A limitation of these studies is that they operate on abstract symbolic inputs ($a \circ b = c$) rather than on visual data, leaving open whether similar algebraic reasoning can emerge from pixel-level observations. \emph{Geometric deep learning} as a unifying framework is surveyed by Bronstein et al.~\cite{bronstein2021geometric}, who formalize symmetry as an inductive bias across architectures; our work contributes an empirical case study in the visual domain.

% --- (5) Project tasks ---
% --- (6) Proposed solution, novelty, advantages ---
We propose a Siamese architecture with a $D_4$-equivariant encoder based on steerable CNNs~\cite{weiler2019general}. Given an image pair $(I_1, I_2)$, the encoder produces embeddings $\mathbf{z}_1, \mathbf{z}_2$; the predicted transformation is identified by applying each of the eight group element representations to~$\mathbf{z}_1$ and selecting the one whose result best aligns with~$\mathbf{z}_2$ by dot-product similarity. Unlike prior grokking studies~\cite{power2022grokking, nanda2023progress} that work with symbolic tokens, our model operates directly on images and must extract the group structure from pixel data. Unlike standard equivariant classification~\cite{cohen2016group, weiler2019general}, we do not only exploit equivariance for feature extraction but require the model to \emph{predict the group element itself}. The novelty of this work is threefold: (i)~we demonstrate that a neural network can learn the $D_4$ multiplication table from visual data, achieving invariance to equivalent operation sequences; (ii)~we provide a controlled ablation comparing standard CNN encoders against $D_4$-equivariant encoders, quantifying the effect of geometric inductive bias on algebraic reasoning; and (iii)~we analyze the learned embedding space and show that its geometry reflects the group-theoretic structure of~$D_4$.

% --- (8) Goal of experiment, setup, datasets, workflow ---
We evaluate the proposed model on image pairs constructed from a standard vision dataset by applying all $D_4$ transformations, supplemented with negative pairs not related by any group element. The experimental workflow consists of three stages: training the Siamese encoder on transformation identification, evaluating accuracy on held-out pairs including equivalent operation sequences and rejection cases, and analyzing the embedding geometry via clustering and distance metrics. We compare $D_4$-equivariant and standard CNN encoders under identical training conditions to isolate the effect of the geometric prior.

% --- Chief Editor formula ---
In summary, this paper proposes a Siamese equivariant architecture for predicting $D_4$ transformations from image pairs, providing evidence that neural networks can discover group-algebraic structure from visual data, and is distinguished from prior work by operating on pixel-level inputs rather than symbolic tokens and by explicitly ablating the role of equivariant inductive bias.

Section~\ref{sec:method} details the architecture and training procedure. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} discusses limitations and future directions.
