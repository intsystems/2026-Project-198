\begin{abstract}
Image plagiarism detection should remain robust to rotations, reflections, and integer shifts of the pixel grid. We ask whether encoding these symmetries as an architectural constraint outperforms approximating them with data augmentation. We train a siamese detector with equivariant ViT encoders under three symmetry groups - $D_4$, $\mathbb{Z}^2$, and $SE(2)$ - combined with an $L^2$ contrastive regularizer, and compare them against an augmented ViT-L/16 baseline on a balanced DomainNet protocol. We report per-transformation FPR and Recall across 10 training runs covering five encoders and two regularization conditions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Image plagiarism detection asks whether two images $x$ and $x'$ depict the same underlying content after one of them has been transformed geometrically or photometrically. The question we study is direct: does building geometric symmetry into the encoder architecture improve plagiarism detection over relying on data augmentation alone? We compare five encoder configurations across three symmetry groups, $D_4$, $\mathbb{Z}^2$, and $SE(2)$, within a single siamese framework, training each with and without an $L^2$ contrastive regularizer on a balanced cross-domain benchmark.

The objects of study are pairs of natural images related by transformations drawn from $D_4$ (rotations by multiples of $90^\circ$ and reflections), $\mathbb{Z}^2$ (integer pixel shifts), or $SE(2)$ (arbitrary rotations and translations), compounded with photometric perturbations such as brightness jitter, contrast scaling, and JPEG compression. Standard Vision Transformers (ViTs)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021image} lack geometric invariance by construction: patch-embedding tokenization is not equivariant to rotations or reflections, and self-attention treats spatial position through learned positional encodings that do not extrapolate well outside the training distribution. A common workaround is geometric augmentation, but augmentation only stochastically covers the orbit of a transformation group, inflates training cost, and offers no invariance guarantee on unseen perturbations. Model scale does not resolve the underlying limitation: recent evidence indicates that spatial and geometric reasoning fails to improve with scale alone~\cite{chen2024spatialvlm}. This motivates embedding the desired symmetry as an inductive bias in the architecture itself, along the lines of geometric deep learning~\cite{bronstein2021geometric,cohen2016group}.

Formally, an encoder $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}} : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Z}$ is \emph{$G$-equivariant} if $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(g \cdot x) = \mathrm{rho}(g)\,\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x)$ for all $g \in G$ and all images $x$, where $\mathrm{rho}$ is a representation of $G$ on the embedding space $\mathcal{Z}$; it is \emph{$G$-invariant} when $\mathrm{rho}$ is the trivial representation, i.e.\ $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(g \cdot x) = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x)$. In plagiarism detection the siamese head compares embeddings $z = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x)$ and $z' = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x')$, so invariance of $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ directly implies that geometric variants of the same image collapse to the same embedding without any augmentation. Full theoretical treatment of these properties is deferred to Section~\ref{sec:prelim}.

Several recent lines of work target geometric robustness in vision transformers. Nordstr\"{o}m et al.~\cite{nordstrom2025octic} introduce the Octic ViT, which enforces exact $D_4$-equivariance through group-structured weight sharing and reduces FLOPs by 40\% relative to a standard ViT of equal expressivity. Rojas-Gomez et al.~\cite{rojasgomez2024shift} design a Shift-Equivariant ViT that achieves exact $\mathbb{Z}^2$-invariance via anti-aliased downsampling and circular padding, but does not address rotational equivariance. Karella et al.~\cite{karella2024harmformer} propose Harmformer, which lifts transformers to the continuous group $SE(2)$ using harmonic feature maps and obtains equivariance to arbitrary in-plane rotations and translations at a higher compute cost. On the side of learned features and plagiarism-specific architectures, Oquab et al.~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} show that DINOv2 pre-training on large unlabeled image collections yields transferable representations, and Dorin et al.~\cite{dorin2024pairwise} establish a siamese ViT baseline for pairwise plagiarism detection with false positive rate (FPR) as the primary evaluation metric~\cite{bromley1993signature}.

We propose a siamese plagiarism detector with $G$-equivariant ViT encoders that provides exact invariance under $D_4$, $\mathbb{Z}^2$, or $SE(2)$ without geometric augmentation. Relative to Dorin et al.~\cite{dorin2024pairwise}, two elements are new: (i) an $L^2$ contrastive regularizer that penalizes the Euclidean distance between embeddings of genuine pairs and pushes impostor pairs beyond a margin, and (ii) a controlled augmentation ablation that pits geometric augmentation against equivariant inductive bias across three symmetry groups in one framework. Concretely, we train five encoder configurations - standard ViT with augmentation, DINOv2-pretrained ViT, Octic ViT ($D_4$), Shift-Equivariant ViT ($\mathbb{Z}^2$), and Harmformer ($SE(2)$) - initialized with DINOv2 weights on ImageNet-1K where architecturally compatible, each in two conditions: with and without the $L^2$ contrastive regularizer.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:rw}

\paragraph{Siamese detectors for image matching and plagiarism.}
Bromley et al.~\cite{bromley1993signature} introduced siamese networks for signature verification: a shared-weight twin architecture with a learned distance metric that extends to any pairwise similarity task. Classical near-duplicate detection relied on compact local descriptors combined with geometric verification~\cite{ke2004efficient}, an approach that breaks under large photometric or domain shifts. Dorin et al.~\cite{dorin2024pairwise} applied a siamese ViT with contrastive loss to image plagiarism detection on DomainNet, reporting FPR as the primary metric and using a cross-batch pairing strategy. We extend their protocol with equivariant encoders and an $L^2$ contrastive regularizer defined on Euclidean distances in the embedding space.

\paragraph{Equivariant and invariant architectures.}
Cohen and Welling~\cite{cohen2016group} introduced G-CNNs that encode discrete symmetries into convolutional filters through group-structured weight sharing. Weiler and Cesa~\cite{weiler2019general} generalised this idea to steerable E(2)-equivariant CNNs, covering continuous rotation and reflection groups within one framework. Bronstein et al.~\cite{bronstein2021geometric} treat equivariance as an inductive bias that improves sample efficiency and generalisation across domains. These architectures have been evaluated mostly on classification and segmentation; to our knowledge, they have not been studied on pairwise image similarity in a siamese plagiarism-detection setting.

\paragraph{Equivariant Vision Transformers.}
Nordstr\"{o}m et al.~\cite{nordstrom2025octic} replace standard ViT layers with $D_4$-equivariant variants, obtaining a 40\% FLOPs reduction at accuracy comparable to the baseline ViT. Rojas-Gomez et al.~\cite{rojasgomez2024shift} enforce exact shift-equivariance through adaptive polyphase sampling, introducing A-token, A-WSA, A-PMerge, and A-RPE modules without adding learnable parameters. Karella et al.~\cite{karella2024harmformer} combine circular harmonic filters with transformer blocks to obtain continuous SE(2)-equivariance across arbitrary rotation angles. None of these architectures has been evaluated on pairwise image similarity or plagiarism detection, which leaves open whether geometric inductive bias reduces false positives on such tasks.

\paragraph{Self-supervised ViT pre-training and contrastive learning.}
DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} produces transferable visual features via self-supervised learning on large unlabeled image collections; we use it as a common pre-training recipe across all encoders so that performance differences reflect architectural choices rather than pre-training data. Chopra et al.~\cite{chopra2005learning} proposed contrastive loss for discriminative metric learning, minimising intra-class distance while maximising inter-class distance. Hadsell et al.~\cite{hadsell2006dimensionality} introduced the $L^2$ contrastive formulation we adopt: the loss depends only on Euclidean distances, so it is preserved under orthogonal transformations of the embedding space.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{sec:problem}
\paragraph{\textbf{1. General problem.}}
Given a pair of images $(x_1, x_2) \in \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X}$, the task is binary
classification: predict $y_{i} \in \{0,1\}$ where $y_{i}=1$ indicates that $x_2$ is a plagiarised
derivative of $x_1$. Plagiarism is operationalised as the composition of a geometric
transformation $g \in G$ with a photometric perturbation $\mathrm{pi} \in G_{\mathrm{photo}}$
(colour jitter, Gaussian blur, additive noise), yielding $x_2 = (g \circ \mathrm{pi})(x_1)$.
The detector must correctly identify such pairs regardless of which transformation was applied.

\paragraph{\textbf{2. Sample set.}}
The labelled dataset is
\[
  \mathcal{D} = \bigl\{(x_{1,i},\, x_{2,i},\, y_{i})\bigr\}_{i=1}^{N},
\]
where $y_{i} \in \{0,1\}$ and every image $x_{i} \in \mathcal{X} = [0,1]^{3 \times 224 \times 224}$.
Each sample is a triple: a query image, a candidate image, and a binary plagiarism label.
The dataset is balanced across positive and negative pairs and stratified by visual domain.

\paragraph{\textbf{3. Statistical hypothesis.}}
Positive pairs are drawn from the joint distribution
\[
  p_+(x_1, x_2) = p(x_1)\, p_T(x_2 \mid x_1),
\]
where $p_T(x_2 \mid x_1) = \int_{G \times G_{\mathrm{photo}}} \mathrm{delta}(x_2 - (g \circ \mathrm{pi})(x_1))\, d\mathrm{mu}(g, \mathrm{pi})$
is the mixture of transformation kernels over $G \cup G_{\mathrm{photo}}$.
Negative pairs are obtained by sampling $x_1$ and $x_2$ independently from the marginal $p(x)$,
so $p_-(x_1, x_2) = p(x_1)\,p(x_2)$.
The detector must distinguish the two regimes on held-out data.

\paragraph{\textbf{4. Measurement conditions.}}
All experiments use the DomainNet dataset~\cite{peng2019domainnet}, which spans six visually
distinct domains: real photograph, painting, clipart, quickdraw, infograph, and sketch.
Images are resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels in 8-bit RGB and normalised per-channel
using ImageNet mean and standard deviation.
This multi-domain structure enables evaluation of generalisation across severe appearance shifts.

\paragraph{\textbf{5. Sample restrictions.}}
Positive pairs consist of a source image $x$ and its transformed copy
$(g \circ \mathrm{pi})(x)$, where $g \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(G)$ and $\mathrm{pi} \sim p_{\mathrm{photo}}$.
Negative pairs are constructed from two independently sampled images drawn from the
\emph{same} DomainNet domain, making them hard negatives with shared visual statistics.
The evaluation set contains 1200 pairs in total: 100 positives and 100 negatives for each
of the six domains, ensuring balanced coverage.

\paragraph{\textbf{6. Model.}}
The full model is $f_{\mathrm{theta}} : \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]$, decomposed as
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_1, x_2) = \mathrm{sigma}\!\bigl(h_{\mathrm{psi}}(\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_1),\, \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_2))\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}} : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ is the encoder with shared weights,
$h_{\mathrm{psi}}$ is the fusion module, and $\mathrm{sigma}$ denotes the sigmoid function.
The siamese design ensures that both images are mapped to the same embedding space before comparison.

\paragraph{\textbf{7. Class restrictions.}}
The encoder $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ must be $G$-equivariant with a $G$-invariant pooling step so that
the resulting embedding satisfies $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(g \cdot x) = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x)$ for all $g \in G$.
This invariance guarantees that geometric variants of the same image collapse to identical embeddings
without data augmentation.
Additionally, the fusion module $h_{\mathrm{psi}}$ must be symmetric:
$h_{\mathrm{psi}}(z_1, z_2) = h_{\mathrm{psi}}(z_2, z_1)$, ensuring that the plagiarism score does not depend
on the ordering of the two inputs.

\paragraph{\textbf{8. Loss / criterion.}}
Training minimises the composite objective
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}} + \mathrm{lambda}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{contr}}^{L^2},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}$ is weighted binary cross-entropy with $w_+ = 0.3$ and $w_- = 0.7$,
and the contrastive regulariser is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{contr}}^{L^2}(z_1, z_2, y_{ij})
    = y_{ij}\,\|z_1 - z_2\|_2^2
      + (1 - y_{ij})\,\max\!\bigl(0,\, m - \|z_1 - z_2\|_2\bigr)^2.
\end{equation}
Setting $\mathrm{lambda} = 0$ recovers the variant trained without contrastive regularisation, which serves
as a baseline to isolate the contribution of the geometric loss term.

\paragraph{\textbf{9. Cross-validation procedure.}}
Generalisation is estimated via stratified five-fold cross-validation over the DomainNet evaluation
pool, with stratification by domain to ensure that every fold contains examples from all six domains.
Hyper-parameters are selected on the validation fold, and test metrics are averaged across folds.
This procedure is applied identically to all encoder configurations to ensure comparability.

\paragraph{\textbf{10. Solution restrictions.}}
All experiments are conducted on a single GPU with at most 48\,GB of VRAM, and fine-tuning is
limited to at most 30 epochs.
The encoder backbone $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ is frozen throughout training; only the fusion head $h_{\mathrm{psi}}$
is updated.
These constraints reflect a realistic resource budget and ensure that differences in performance
reflect architectural choices rather than training cost.

\paragraph{\textbf{11. External quality criteria.}}
The primary metrics are \textbf{FPR} (false positive rate at the threshold that maximises F1 on the
validation fold) and \textbf{Recall} reported per transformation class: colour jitter (CJ),
Gaussian noise (GN), Gaussian blur (GS), rotation by $90^\circ$ (R90), $180^\circ$ (R180),
$270^\circ$ (R270), watermark (WM), and combinations thereof.
Secondary metrics are F1 score and t-SNE separability of embeddings $z$ on DomainNet.
Stratified recall directly measures whether a given encoder is robust to specific transformation types.

\paragraph{\textbf{12. Argmin.}}
The optimal encoder and fusion parameters are given by
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathrm{theta}} = \arg\min_{\mathrm{theta}}\;
    \mathbb{E}_{(x_1,\, x_2,\, y_{i})\,\sim\,\mathcal{D}}\,
    \mathcal{L}(\mathrm{theta}).
\end{equation}
In practice, this expectation is approximated by the empirical mean over mini-batches,
and $\hat{\mathrm{theta}}$ is taken as the checkpoint with the lowest validation loss across the
five cross-validation folds.

\section{Preliminaries: Equivariance}\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Group Actions and Representations}

\begin{definition}[Group action]
A \emph{left action} of a group $G$ on a set $\mathcal{X}$ is a map
$G \times \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{X}$,
$(g, x) \mapsto g \cdot x$,
satisfying $e \cdot x = x$ and $(gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)$
for all $g, h \in G$, $x \in \mathcal{X}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Group representation]
A \emph{(linear) representation} of $G$ on a vector space $V$ is a group homomorphism
$\mathrm{rho} \colon G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$.
\end{definition}

A representation makes concrete how $G$ acts on a feature space: each group element $g$ is assigned an invertible linear map $\mathrm{rho}(g)$ on $V$, and group multiplication is preserved by $\mathrm{rho}(gh) = \mathrm{rho}(g)\mathrm{rho}(h)$.

\subsection{Equivariance and Invariance}

\begin{definition}[$G$-Equivariance]
A map $\mathrm{phi} \colon \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ is \emph{$G$-equivariant} with respect to
representations $\mathrm{rho}_\mathcal{X}$ on $\mathcal{X}$ and $\mathrm{rho}_\mathcal{Y}$ on $\mathcal{Y}$ if
\[
  \mathrm{phi}\bigl(\mathrm{rho}_\mathcal{X}(g) \cdot x\bigr) = \mathrm{rho}_\mathcal{Y}(g) \cdot \mathrm{phi}(x)
  \quad \forall\, g \in G,\; x \in \mathcal{X}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[$G$-Invariance]
$\mathrm{phi}$ is \emph{$G$-invariant} if $\mathrm{rho}_\mathcal{Y}$ is the trivial representation:
$\mathrm{phi}(\mathrm{rho}_\mathcal{X}(g) \cdot x) = \mathrm{phi}(x)$ for all $g \in G$.
\end{definition}

Invariance is thus a special case of equivariance in which the output space carries only the trivial action of $G$.
For plagiarism detection, a $G$-invariant encoder $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ satisfies $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(g \cdot x) = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x)$ for every $g \in G$, so embeddings are identical regardless of which group element was applied to the source image.
This removes the need for group-specific augmentation at training time and yields an exact invariance guarantee at inference time.

\subsection{Irreducible Representations and Schur's Lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Schur]
Let $\mathrm{rho}_1 \colon G \to \mathrm{GL}(V_1)$ and $\mathrm{rho}_2 \colon G \to \mathrm{GL}(V_2)$ be
irreducible representations of a group $G$.
Any $G$-equivariant linear map $\mathrm{phi} \colon V_1 \to V_2$ is either the zero map or
an isomorphism.
\end{lemma}

A consequence of Schur's lemma is that $G$-equivariant linear maps between fully decomposed representations are block-diagonal with respect to the irreducible decomposition: each block acts within one irreducible component and cannot mix distinct irreducible types.
This constraint shrinks the parameter space of equivariant layers relative to unconstrained linear maps, and it motivates the architecture of Octic ViT~\cite{nordstrom2025octic}, where each transformer layer operates independently on each irreducible component of $D_4$ so the block-diagonal structure holds by construction.

\subsection{Symmetry Groups in This Work}

\paragraph{The dihedral group $D_4$.}
$D_4 = \langle r, s \mid r^4 = s^2 = e,\; srs = r^{-1} \rangle$ is the symmetry group of the square, of order~8.
It acts on images by rotating and reflecting the spatial axes by multiples of $90^\circ$.
$D_4$ has five irreducible representations over $\mathbb{R}$: four one-dimensional ($A_1, A_2, B_1, B_2$) and one two-dimensional ($E$); any $D_4$-representation decomposes as $V \cong \bigoplus_i n_i V_i$ over these five types.

\paragraph{The discrete translation group $\mathbb{Z}^2$.}
The group $(\mathbb{Z}^2, +)$ acts on an $H \times W$ image by cyclic pixel shifts:
$(t_{a,b} \cdot x)[i, j, c] = x[(i + a) \bmod H,\; (j + b) \bmod W,\; c]$.
This action is relevant when plagiarists crop and re-centre images.
In contrast to $D_4$, the group $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is infinite (or large-but-finite on discrete images), and exact equivariance is harder to enforce in the ViT patch-based setting.

\paragraph{The special Euclidean group $SE(2)$.}
$SE(2) = SO(2) \ltimes \mathbb{R}^2$ is the group of rigid motions of the plane: an element $(R_{\mathrm{alpha}}, t)$ rotates spatial coordinates by angle $\mathrm{alpha}$ and translates by $t \in \mathbb{R}^2$.
Because $D_4$ covers only multiples of $90^\circ$ while $SE(2)$ admits arbitrary rotation angles, $SE(2)$-equivariance is more general but also harder to realise exactly on a discrete pixel grid.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Siamese Framework}

The detector follows the siamese architecture of Bromley et al.~\cite{bromley1993signature}. Two branches share one encoder $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ and process the query image $x_1$ and the candidate image $x_2$ in parallel to produce embeddings $z_1 = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_1)$ and $z_2 = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_2)$. A fusion module $h_{\mathrm{psi}}$ combines them into a scalar score, and a sigmoid maps the score to a plagiarism probability. The full model (already introduced in \S\ref{sec:problem}) is
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_1, x_2) = \mathrm{sigma}\!\bigl(h_{\mathrm{psi}}(\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_1),\, \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_2))\bigr).
\end{equation}
Weight sharing maps $x_1$ and $x_2$ into the same feature space; without it the two branches would produce vectors in unrelated coordinate systems and any pairwise comparison would be ill-defined.

\subsection{Encoders}

\paragraph{ViT-L/16 (augmented).}
The baseline encoder is a standard ViT-L/16~\cite{dosovitskiy2021image}. Each $224 \times 224$ input is split into $14 \times 14 = 196$ non-overlapping $16 \times 16$ patches; each patch is linearly projected to $\mathbb{R}^{1024}$, and the resulting sequence, together with a prepended \texttt{[CLS]} token, is passed through 24 transformer blocks with multi-head self-attention. The \texttt{[CLS]} token after the final layer norm is taken as the image embedding $z \in \mathbb{R}^{1024}$. This encoder has no equivariance guarantee. At training time it receives both photometric augmentations (block P) and geometric augmentations (block G); see \S\ref{sec:aug}.

\paragraph{ViT-L/16 (baseline).}
Architecturally identical to ViT-L/16 (augmented), but trained with block P only; block G geometric transforms are withheld. This is the key ablation: if the augmentation-free variant matches the equivariant encoders, then geometric augmentation alone suffices and architectural inductive bias adds nothing beyond what augmentation already provides.

\paragraph{Octic ViT ($D_4$-equivariant).}
Nordstr\"{o}m et al.~\cite{nordstrom2025octic} replace each ViT layer with a $D_4$-equivariant variant. By Schur's lemma (\S\ref{sec:prelim}), equivariant linear maps act independently on each irreducible component, so the embedding space decomposes as
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{R}^d \;\cong\;
  \underbrace{(\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{A_1} \oplus (\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{A_2}
              \oplus (\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{B_1} \oplus (\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{B_2}}_{\text{four 1-dim irreps}}
  \oplus
  \underbrace{(\mathbb{R}^{d/4})_E}_{\text{one 2-dim irrep}}.
\end{equation}
Invariant pooling projects onto the trivial component $A_1$ to give a $D_4$-invariant output $z$. Trained with block P only.

\paragraph{Shift-Equivariant ViT ($\mathbb{Z}^2$-equivariant).}
Rojas-Gomez et al.~\cite{rojasgomez2024shift} enforce exact cyclic shift-equivariance by replacing four ViT modules with adaptive polyphase variants: an A-token module (per-sample polyphase offset selection), A-WSA (adaptive window self-attention partition), A-PMerge (polyphase downsampling), and A-RPE (circular relative position encoding). The four modifications yield exact shift-equivariance without introducing extra learnable parameters. Trained with block P only.

\paragraph{Harmformer ($SE(2)$-equivariant).}
Karella et al.~\cite{karella2024harmformer} combine circular harmonic filters (steerable feature maps) with transformer self-attention blocks to obtain equivariance under continuous roto-translations. Compared with Octic ViT, which is restricted to $90^\circ$ multiples of the dihedral group, Harmformer covers arbitrary rotation angles; the stronger guarantee comes at a higher computational cost. Trained with block P only.

All encoders are pre-trained on ImageNet-1K using the DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} self-supervised recipe.

\subsection{Fusion Module}

The fusion module combines two embeddings $z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{R}^d$ into a scalar via a two-layer MLP applied to element-wise features:
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mathrm{psi}}(z_1, z_2) = W_2\,\mathrm{ReLU}\!\bigl(W_1\,[|z_1 - z_2|\;\|\;z_1 \odot z_2] + b_1\bigr) + b_2,
\end{equation}
where $|\cdot|$ denotes element-wise absolute value, $\odot$ element-wise product, and $[\cdot\|\cdot]$ concatenation.

\begin{proposition}[Symmetry of the fusion module]
$h_{\mathrm{psi}}(z_1, z_2) = h_{\mathrm{psi}}(z_2, z_1)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$|z_1 - z_2| = |z_2 - z_1|$ and $z_1 \odot z_2 = z_2 \odot z_1$;
therefore the concatenated input to the MLP is the same under transposition.
\end{proof}

\subsection{$L^2$ Contrastive Regularizer}

The contrastive regularizer (introduced in \S\ref{sec:problem}) is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{contr}^{L^2}(z_1, z_2, y_{ij})
    = y_{ij}\,\|z_1 - z_2\|_2^2
      + (1 - y_{ij})\,\max\!\bigl(0,\, m - \|z_1 - z_2\|_2\bigr)^2.
\end{equation}

We use $L^2$ distance rather than cosine similarity because it is better aligned with the group-invariant geometry of the embedding space. Following Hadsell et al.~\cite{hadsell2006dimensionality}, the quantity $\|z_1 - z_2\|_2$ is an \emph{isometric invariant}: it is preserved by any orthogonal transformation of $\mathbb{R}^d$. This matches $G$-invariant encoders that may differ only by an isometry of the embedding space. Cosine similarity, in contrast, depends on vector norms, so it is not preserved by general orthogonal maps.

\begin{proposition}[Isometry invariance of $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{contr}^{L^2}$]
Let $U \colon \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ be an orthogonal map.
Then $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{contr}^{L^2}(Uz_1, Uz_2, y_{ij}) = \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{contr}^{L^2}(z_1, z_2, y_{ij})$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\|Uz_1 - Uz_2\|_2 = \|U(z_1 - z_2)\|_2 = \|z_1 - z_2\|_2$,
since orthogonal maps preserve the $L^2$ norm.
\end{proof}

For each encoder we compare two conditions: $\mathrm{lambda} > 0$ (with $L^2$ regularizer) and $\mathrm{lambda} = 0$ (without).

Equivariant encoders by design produce the same embedding for any $g \in G$ applied to the input, so block G augmentation on those encoders generates pairs with zero gradient for the head: harmless but redundant. The experimental hypothesis is therefore that block G can be dropped for equivariant encoders without degrading detection performance.

\subsection{Theoretical Properties: Invariance Propagation}

\begin{proposition}[Invariance propagation]
If $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ is $G$-invariant, then $f_{\mathrm{theta}}(g \cdot x_1, g \cdot x_2) = f_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_1, x_2)$
for all $g \in G$ and all $x_1, x_2 \in \mathcal{X}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$G$-invariance of $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}$ gives $\mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(g \cdot x_i) = \mathrm{phi}_{\mathrm{theta}}(x_i)$ for $i = 1, 2$.
Since $h_{\mathrm{psi}}$ and $\mathrm{sigma}$ are deterministic functions of their inputs, the output of $f_{\mathrm{theta}}$ is unchanged.
\end{proof}

The plagiarism score is thus the same for every $g \in G$ applied jointly to both inputs, which is the invariance required for geometric plagiarism detection.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Dataset and Pre-training}

All encoders are pre-trained on COCO2017 with the DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} self-supervised recipe.
Pre-training is done separately per encoder architecture when DINOv2-compatible weights exist; for architectures without publicly available DINOv2 checkpoints, we initialize from a ViT-L/16 DINOv2 checkpoint and adapt the weights.

Evaluation uses the DomainNet benchmark~\cite{peng2019domainnet}, which contains images from six visually diverse domains: real photograph, painting, clipart, quickdraw, infograph, and sketch.
We build a balanced test set of 1200 pairs: 100 positive and 100 negative pairs per domain (600 positives and 600 negatives in total).
Each image is resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels, giving $3 \times 224 \times 224 = 150{,}528$ scalar features per image.
A positive pair is a source image together with a transformed copy; a negative pair is two distinct images drawn from the same domain (hard negatives).

\subsection{Training Pipeline}

The experiment consists of 10 training runs: 5 encoders $\times$ \{$\mathrm{lambda} = 0$ (without $L^2$ regulariser), $\mathrm{lambda} > 0$ (with $L^2$ regulariser)\}.

In each run we load base images from the training split and build pairs via the cross-batch strategy of Dorin et al.~\cite{dorin2024pairwise}: a batch of $B$ images is augmented to form two views per image (block P for all encoders, and additionally block G for ViT-L/16 augmented), and all $B^2$ pairwise combinations are formed with labels $y_{ij} = \mathbb{1}[i = j]$.
Embeddings are computed through the frozen encoder and the fusion module, the composite loss $\mathcal{L}$ is evaluated, and AdamW updates the fusion parameters $\mathrm{psi}$.
Hyperparameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

For each (encoder, $\pm L^2$) run, we train on the training split, pick a decision threshold on the validation fold by maximising F1, and then compute final metrics on the held-out test set.

Primary metrics are false positive rate (FPR) and Recall, both stratified by transformation class: ColorJitter (CJ), Gaussian Noise (GN), Grayscale (GS), R90, R180, R270, Watermark (WM), and their pairwise combinations.
FPR is reported for comparability with Dorin et al.~\cite{dorin2024pairwise}.
Secondary metrics include the F1 score and a t-SNE visualisation of encoder embeddings on the full DomainNet evaluation set, with the six domains shown as distinct point colours.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:concl}
We presented a siamese plagiarism detector that benchmarks five encoder configurations
spanning three symmetry groups, $D_4$ (Octic ViT), $\mathbb{Z}^2$ (Shift-Equivariant ViT),
and $SE(2)$ (Harmformer), against a plain ViT-L/16 baseline.
Fixing the DINOv2 pre-training protocol and varying only the geometric inductive bias and
the presence of an $L^2$ contrastive regularizer lets the framework separate the
contribution of built-in $G$-invariance from that of data augmentation across 10 runs.
Section~\ref{sec:method} establishes three properties of the setup:
symmetry of the fusion module, isometry invariance of the $L^2$ contrastive loss,
and invariance propagation through the full siamese pipeline.
Whether $G$-invariant encoders trained without block G augmentations
match or exceed a ViT-L/16 trained with full augmentation is the empirical question
left to the DomainNet evaluation.

The experimental runs (\S\ref{sec:results}) will be completed in subproject C:
implementing Shift-Equivariant ViT and Harmformer, pre-training all five encoders on
ImageNet-1K, and executing the 10 siamese fine-tuning experiments.
We will report FPR and Recall stratified by transformation class on the
balanced DomainNet test set.
We expect the equivariant encoders to support the inductive bias hypothesis:
reduced reliance on geometric augmentation with detection performance preserved.
