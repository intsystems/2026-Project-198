\begin{abstract}
A pirated image is rarely a pixel-perfect copy: rotations, reflections, crops,
and re-compression are routine, and a detector should map all of them to the same
point in feature space. We ask whether building these symmetries into the encoder of a
siamese plagiarism detector is preferable to approximating them with data augmentation,
and we resolve the question separately for the dihedral group of the square and for the
special Euclidean group of the plane. Three encoders are compared inside one pipeline: a
non-equivariant ViT-Tiny baseline trained with group-specific geometric augmentation, an
Octic ViT with exact dihedral invariance, and a Harmformer with continuous roto-translational
invariance. Training pairs are formed on COCO with a cross-batch protocol; evaluation uses
cross-domain pairs from DomainNet, in an overall regime under the training-time augmentation
distribution and a per-stratum regime that isolates one geometric transformation at a time,
both at a fixed threshold. For the dihedral group, the architecturally invariant Octic ViT
trained without geometric augmentation matches the augmented baseline on every dihedral
transformation and roughly triples its precision on negatives, which supports the
equivariance hypothesis. For the special Euclidean group, the verdict is mixed: the
Harmformer improves overall metrics over the augmented baseline and is exact on
right-angle rotations and reflections, but collapses on off-axis rotations and on
translation, which we attribute to interpolation noise at low input resolution rather
than to the equivariance principle.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} image plagiarism detection; siamese networks; equivariant Vision Transformers;
 group invariance; contrastive learning; DomainNet.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Two images depict the same content under a plagiarist's edits when one differs from the other by a rotation, a reflection, a crop, or a photometric perturbation, and a detector should map both to the same point in feature space. The question we study is whether to enforce this invariance through the encoder architecture or to approximate it through geometric augmentation at training time. 

Standard Vision Transformers~\citep{dosovitskiy2021image} provide no geometric guarantee. Patch tokenization commutes with neither rotation nor reflection, and learned positional encodings do not extrapolate beyond the training distribution; geometric augmentation can only cover the orbit of a transformation group stochastically, inflates training cost, and makes no promise on transformations unseen during training. Scale alone does not close this gap, since spatial reasoning of large vision models has been shown to fail to improve with parameter count~\citep{chen2024spatialvlm}. Geometric deep learning~\citep{bronstein2021geometric, cohen2016group, weiler2019general} formalizes the alternative, in which the desired invariance is built into the architecture as commutation with a group action and then propagates exactly through the network. The siamese template, on which the detector is built, is due to~\citet{bromley1993signature}.

Classical near-duplicate pipelines~\citep{ke2004efficient} embed geometric invariance directly in the matching procedure and therefore tolerate rotations, reflections and crops by construction, but lose accuracy under domain shift and photometric edits. Modern self-supervised descriptors for image copy detection~\citep{pizzi2022sscd, kim2025rdcd} reverse this profile: they are robust to photometric perturbations and domain shift and define the state of the art on standard copy-detection benchmarks, yet carry no architectural geometric guarantee and depend on augmentation to approximate it. SSCD~\citep{pizzi2022sscd} couples a SimCLR-style contrastive objective on a convolutional backbone with an entropy regularizer that enforces a consistent separation of descriptor vectors across the embedding space and with augmentations that mix pairs of images at training time, and it replaces the standard pooling head with a generalized-mean operator adapted from instance retrieval; the resulting compact descriptor improves over a SimCLR baseline by 48 absolute points of micro average precision on DISC2021. RDCD~\citep{kim2025rdcd} treats descriptor compactness as a distillation problem: a frozen SSCD-style teacher defines a pairwise similarity structure over an instance queue, and a smaller student is trained to reproduce that structure through a Kullback-Leibler term combined with a hard-negative loss, which yields 64-, 128- and 256-dimensional descriptors that match or exceed the teacher on DISC2021. In both pipelines the robustness to rotation, reflection and crop is sampled rather than guaranteed and remains bounded by the empirical augmentation distribution, which is the gap that an architecturally $G$-invariant encoder closes by construction. Among architectural alternatives, the Octic ViT~\citep{nordstrom2025octic} guarantees invariance to rotations by multiples of 90 degrees and to reflections, but covers no other angle, while Harmformer~\citep{karella2024harmformer} extends the guarantee to all rotation angles at a higher compute cost. None of these architectures has been evaluated on pairwise plagiarism detection, which is the specific gap we address.

The contribution is a single siamese pipeline that wraps three encoders, a non-equivariant ViT-Tiny/16 baseline, an Octic ViT, and a Harmformer, and pits architectural group invariance against geometric augmentation under matched conditions. We utilize a controlled augmentation ablation: each equivariant encoder is trained without group augmentation, while the ViT-Tiny/16 baseline is trained with the corresponding group-specific augmentation block, so that any difference in error attributes to architectural inductive bias rather than to data exposure. The computational experiment estimates whether group-invariant encoders trained without the group augmentation block match or exceed the augmented baseline on false positive rate and per-class recall on a balanced cross-domain test of 1\,200 pairs sampled from DomainNet~\citep{peng2019domainnet}; training pairs are constructed on COCO\,2017 with the cross-batch protocol of~\citet{dorin2024pairwise}.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:rw}

Twin networks with shared weights and a learned distance were introduced by~\citet{bromley1993signature} for signature verification and have since been adopted across pairwise matching problems. Classical near-duplicate detection combined local descriptors with geometric verification~\citep{ke2004efficient}, which gave geometric robustness by construction but was brittle under domain shift and photometric edits, where modern visual features are stronger. \citet{dorin2024pairwise} applied a siamese ViT with a contrastive loss to image plagiarism on DomainNet and proposed the cross-batch pairing protocol that we reuse without modification; the pipeline reports FPR as the primary metric, but offers no invariance guarantee against geometric perturbations and depends on geometric augmentation for robustness.

Group-equivariant convolutional networks~\citep{cohen2016group} encode discrete symmetries through weight sharing on group orbits, and \citet{weiler2019general} extend the construction to the continuous Euclidean group $E(2)$ via steerable filters. \citet{bronstein2021geometric} treat equivariance as an inductive bias that improves sample efficiency under group-structured data, and the same line of reasoning motivates the transformer variants used here. The Octic ViT~\citep{nordstrom2025octic} replaces standard transformer layers with $D_4$-equivariant ones; by Schur's lemma, equivariant linear maps decompose blockwise across the five irreducible representations of $D_4$, which both constrains parameters and reduces FLOPs by 40\% at expressivity comparable to a non-equivariant ViT, but the resulting invariance is restricted to multiples of $90^\circ$ and reflections. Harmformer~\citep{karella2024harmformer} replaces patch projections with circular harmonic filters and combines them with self-attention to obtain continuous roto-translational equivariance over $SE(2)$, which covers every rotation angle but at a higher compute cost than $D_4$. Neither architecture has been evaluated on pairwise plagiarism detection, which leaves open whether the geometric inductive bias they provide actually reduces false positives on this specific task.

The contrastive component of our loss follows the formulation of~\citet{hadsell2006dimensionality} and the metric-learning template of~\citet{chopra2005learning}: it depends only on Euclidean distances between embeddings, which means that an orthogonal change of basis on the embedding space leaves it invariant, and that property aligns the loss geometry with the geometry of $G$-invariant encoders.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{sec:problem}

We treat image plagiarism detection as a binary classification problem on image pairs: given a pair $(x_1, x_2)\in\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{X}$, predict $y \in \{0,1\}$, where $y=1$ marks $x_2$ as a plagiarized derivative of $x_1$. 

Plagiarism is operationalized as the composition of a geometric transformation $g \in G$ with a photometric perturbation $\eta\in\mathcal{P}$, producing $x_2 = (g \circ \eta)(x_1)$. 

The geometric component $g$ is drawn from one of the symmetry groups $G \in \{D_4, SE(2)\}$.

$D_4$ is the dihedral group of the square:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:d4-def}
  D_4 = \{ r, s \mid r^4 = s^2 = e,\ srs = r^{-1}\},
\end{equation}
of order eight, where $r$ is the rotation of the image plane by $90^\circ$ and $s$ is reflection across a fixed axis.

$SE(2)$ is the special Euclidean group of the plane is the semidirect product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:se2-def}
  SE(2) = SO(2) \ltimes \mathbb{R}^2 = \{(R_\alpha, \mathbf{t})\mid \alpha\in[0, 2\pi),\ \mathbf{t}\in\mathbb{R}^2\},
\end{equation}
acting on coordinates by $(R_\alpha, \mathbf{t})\cdot\mathbf{x} = R_\alpha\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}$. 

The photometric component $\eta$ is drawn from the set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:photo-set}
  \mathcal{P} = \{\mathrm{CJ},\ \mathrm{GBlur},\ \mathrm{GN},\ \mathrm{JPEG},\ \mathrm{WM}\},
\end{equation}
 where $\mathrm{CJ}$ is brightness and contrast jitter, $\mathrm{GBlur}$ is Gaussian blur, $\mathrm{GN}$ is additive Gaussian noise, $\mathrm{JPEG}$ is JPEG re-compression and $\mathrm{WM}$ is a watermark overlay; the set is shared across all training and evaluation regimes.

The dataset is $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_{1,i}, x_{2,i}, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ with $x_{\cdot,i} \in \mathcal{X} = [0,1]^{C\times H\times W}$ and $y_i \in \{0,1\}$, balanced between positive and negative pairs and stratified by visual domain.

Let $\phi^G,\phi^{\mathrm{aug}}\colon\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{X}\to\{0,1\}$, where $\phi^G$ is built around a $G$-invariant ViT trained without block-$G$ augmentation and $\phi^{\mathrm{aug}}$ around a non-equivariant ViT trained with block-$G$ augmentation. For every transformation $T\in G$ we test
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hypothesis}
  \mathrm{FPR}_T(\phi^G)\le \mathrm{FPR}_T(\phi^{\mathrm{aug}})
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \mathrm{Recall}_T(\phi^G)\ge \mathrm{Recall}_T(\phi^{\mathrm{aug}}).
\end{equation}

\section{Preliminaries: Equivariance}\label{sec:prelim}

A left action of a group $G$ on a set $\mathcal{X}$ is a map $G\times\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{X}$, $(g, x)\mapsto g\cdot x$, satisfying $e\cdot x = x$ and $(gh)\cdot x = g\cdot(h\cdot x)$ for all $g, h\in G$ and all $x\in\mathcal{X}$. A linear representation of $G$ on a vector space $V$ is a homomorphism $\rho\colon G\to\mathrm{GL}(V)$, so that group multiplication is preserved by $\rho(gh) = \rho(g)\rho(h)$. A subspace $W\subseteq V$ is $G$-invariant if $\rho(g)W\subseteq W$ for all $g\in G$, and the representation is irreducible when its only $G$-invariant subspaces are $\{0\}$ and $V$. A map $\phi\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathbb{R}^d$ is $G$-equivariant with respect to representations $\rho_{\mathcal{X}}$ on $\mathcal{X}$ and $\rho$ on $\mathbb{R}^d$ if $\phi(\rho_{\mathcal{X}}(g)\,x) = \rho(g)\,\phi(x)$ for every $g\in G$ and every $x\in\mathcal{X}$, and it is $G$-invariant when $\rho$ is the trivial representation, that is, when $\phi(g\cdot x) = \phi(x)$. For plagiarism detection, $G$-invariance of the encoder $\phi_\theta$ implies that all geometric variants of an image collapse to a single embedding without augmentation, so the head receives identical inputs whether or not a $g\in G$ has been applied; the detection problem reduces to comparing two points whose distance no longer depends on the geometric component of the perturbation.

\begin{lemma}[Schur]\label{lem:schur}
Let $(V, \rho_V)$ and $(W, \rho_W)$ be finite-dimensional irreducible representations of a group $G$ over an algebraically closed field, and let $T\colon V\to W$ be a $G$-equivariant linear map, that is,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schur-intertwine}
  T\,\rho_V(g) = \rho_W(g)\,T \qquad\text{for every } g\in G.
\end{equation}
Then either $T = 0$, or $T$ is an isomorphism. In the latter case $V$ and $W$ are equivalent as representations, and if $V = W$ and $\rho_V = \rho_W$, then $T = \lambda\,\mathrm{id}_V$ for some scalar $\lambda$.
\end{lemma}

A direct consequence is that any equivariant linear map between fully decomposed representations is block-diagonal across the irreducible decomposition: if $V = \bigoplus_{i} V_i^{\oplus n_i}$ and $W = \bigoplus_{i} W_i^{\oplus m_i}$ are decomposed into isotypic components indexed by the irreducibles $V_i$ of $G$, then a $G$-equivariant linear map $T\colon V\to W$ acts within each isotypic component and cannot mix distinct irreducible types. The Octic ViT~\citep{nordstrom2025octic} relies on this property, since each transformer layer operates independently on each irreducible component of $D_4$, so the block-diagonal structure holds by construction and the parameter count drops without loss of expressivity inside each isotypic component.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Siamese pipeline}

The detector follows the siamese template of~\citet{bromley1993signature}. The detector is the composition $f_\theta(x_1, x_2) = \sigma\bigl(h_\psi(\phi_\theta(x_1), \phi_\theta(x_2))\bigr)$, where $\phi_\theta\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathbb{R}^d$ is a shared encoder, $h_\psi$ is a fusion head, and $\sigma$ is the sigmoid.

\subsection{Encoders}

The non-equivariant reference is a ViT-Tiny/16~\citep{dosovitskiy2021image}. A $224\times 224$ input is split into $14\times 14 = 196$ non-overlapping $16\times 16$ patches, each linearly projected to $\mathbb{R}^{192}$, and the resulting sequence with a prepended \texttt{[CLS]} token is processed by twelve transformer blocks with multi-head self-attention. The \texttt{[CLS]} embedding after the final layer norm is taken as the image representation $z\in\mathbb{R}^{192}$. The encoder has no equivariance guarantee, and at training time it receives both the photometric block and a group-specific geometric block matched to the comparison in which it participates.

The Octic ViT~\citep{nordstrom2025octic} replaces each transformer layer with a $D_4$-equivariant variant. By Schur's lemma the embedding space decomposes across the irreducibles of $D_4$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:octic-decomp}
  \mathbb{R}^d \;\cong\;
  (\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{A_1}\oplus(\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{A_2}\oplus(\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{B_1}\oplus(\mathbb{R}^{d/8})_{B_2}
  \;\oplus\;(\mathbb{R}^{d/4})_{E},
\end{equation}
into four one-dimensional isotypic components and one two-dimensional component. $D_4$-invariant pooling projects onto the trivial isotypic component $A_1$ to produce a $D_4$-invariant output.

Harmformer~\citep{karella2024harmformer} replaces patch embeddings with circular harmonic filters that act as steerable feature maps and combines them with self-attention to obtain equivariance under continuous roto-translations.

\subsection{Fusion head}

The head takes $z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and returns a logit through pair features and a two-layer MLP. Set $\delta = |z_1 - z_2|$ for the element-wise absolute difference, $p = z_1\odot z_2$ for the element-wise product, and $v = [u_\delta(\delta);\, u_p(p)]\in\mathbb{R}^{2d}$ for the concatenation after independent linear maps; the head value is then $h_\psi(z_1, z_2) = c^\top \mathrm{ReLU}(A v + b)$. 

Since $|z_1 - z_2| = |z_2 - z_1|$ and $z_1\odot z_2 = z_2\odot z_1$, the head is symmetric in its two arguments.

\subsection{Data sampling strategy}\label{sec:data-sampling}

Training pairs are constructed with the cross-batch protocol of~\citet{dorin2024pairwise}. A mini-batch of $B$ source images $\{x^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{B}$ is sampled uniformly without replacement from the training pool, and each image is mapped to two branches: an anchor branch $x^{(i)}_1$, on which only the geometry-preserving resize to a fixed input size is applied, and an augmented branch $x^{(i)}_2$, which additionally passes through the photometric block (and, for the non-equivariant baseline, the geometric block of Section~\ref{sec:aug}). The pair $(x^{(i)}_1, x^{(i)}_2)$ is therefore a clean image and one of its augmented derivatives, which is the unit of positive supervision the detector should map together. Cross-batch pairing then reuses the same two branches across the whole mini-batch: every ordered pair $(x^{(i)}_1, x^{(j)}_2)$ with $i, j\in\{1, \ldots, B\}$ is included as a training example with label
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cross-batch-label}
  y_{ij} \;=\; \mathbbm{1}[i = j],
\end{equation}
so that one mini-batch produces $B^2$ pairs, of which $B$ are positive ($y_{ii} = 1$) and $B^2 - B$ are negative. The protocol thus emits a label-imbalanced stream with positive-to-negative ratio $1 : (B-1)$, which motivates the class weights $w_+, w_-$ in~\eqref{eq:loss-bce}. To prevent gradient overflow under mixed-precision training we adopt a square-root balance, $w_+ / w_- = \sqrt{B-1}$, with $w_- = 1$.

\subsection{Loss function}

The total loss function over a labelled dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_{1,i}, x_{2,i}, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss-total}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}}(\mathcal{D}) \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Bigl[\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}(x_{1,i}, x_{2,i}, y_i) + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{contr}}(x_{1,i}, x_{2,i}, y_i)\Bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\lambda > 0$ is the regularization weight. The binary cross-entropy term acts on the predicted plagiarism probability $\hat y_i = f_\theta(x_{1,i}, x_{2,i})$ produced by the detector,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss-bce}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}(x_1, x_2, y) \;=\; -\bigl[\,w_+\,y\,\log\hat y \;+\; w_-\,(1-y)\,\log(1 - \hat y)\,\bigr], \qquad \hat y = f_\theta(x_1, x_2),
\end{equation}
with class weights $w_+, w_- > 0$ that compensate for the label imbalance induced by the cross-batch pairing of Section~\ref{sec:data-sampling}. The contrastive term acts on the encoder embeddings $z_i = \phi_\theta(x_i)\in\mathbb{R}^d$ through the squared $L^2$ distance,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss-contr}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{contr}}(x_1, x_2, y) \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \|\phi_\theta(x_1) - \phi_\theta(x_2)\|_2^2, & \text{if } y = 1,\\[2pt]
    \max\bigl(0,\, m - \|\phi_\theta(x_1) - \phi_\theta(x_2)\|_2\bigr)^2, & \text{if } y = 0,
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
with contrastive margin $m > 0$. On a positive pair the term penalizes any non-zero distance between the two embeddings and drives them together; on a negative pair it penalizes embeddings that lie within the margin and drives them apart, vanishing once the $L^2$ distance reaches~$m$.

\subsection{Augmentation blocks}\label{sec:aug}

The augmentation pipeline consists of a photometric block applied to all encoders and a geometric block added only to the non-equivariant ViT-Tiny/16 baseline. Inside each block the individual transformations are drawn independently for every pair, and each one is applied with probability $p = 0.3$.

The photometric block draws its elements from the set $\mathcal{P}$ in~\eqref{eq:photo-set}. When sampled, $\mathrm{CJ}$ jitters brightness, contrast and saturation with strength $0.3$ and hue with strength $0.1$, with each channel offset drawn uniformly from the corresponding range; $\mathrm{GBlur}$ convolves the image with a $3\times 3$ Gaussian kernel of standard deviation $\sigma\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0.1, 2.0]$; $\mathrm{GN}$ adds independent zero-mean Gaussian noise to every pixel with standard deviation $\sigma\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0.01, 0.05]$; $\mathrm{JPEG}$ re-encodes the image at integer quality $q\sim\mathrm{Unif}\{10, \ldots, 50\}$; and $\mathrm{WM}$ overlays a randomly placed text watermark with transparency $\alpha\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0.1, 0.4]$.

The geometric block is matched to the symmetry group of the comparison. In the $D_4$ comparison it samples, each with probability $0.3$, a discrete rotation by $90^\circ k$ with $k\in\{1, 2, 3\}$ drawn uniformly, a horizontal flip and a vertical flip. In the $SE(2)$ comparison it samples, each with probability $0.3$, a continuous rotation with angle $\alpha\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0, 2\pi)$ and an isotropic random translation with displacement on each axis drawn uniformly from $\pm 20\%$ of the corresponding image extent.

\paragraph{Test set construction.} The test pool is sampled from DomainNet~\citep{peng2019domainnet}, which spans six visually diverse domains: real, painting, clipart, quickdraw, infograph and sketch. From each domain we pre-select $200$ source images, giving $1\,200$ source images in total. Two pair regimes are built on top of this pool. The \emph{overall} regime forms a single balanced test set of $1\,200$ pairs, $600$ positive and $600$ negative ($100$ positive and $100$ negative per domain), with both branches passed through the same training-time augmentation pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:aug} (the photometric block for every model, plus the geometric block matched to the model's training group), so that the global $\mathrm{FPR}$ and $\mathrm{Recall}$ are measured on data drawn from the training distribution. The \emph{per-stratum} regime forms one separate test set of $1\,200$ pairs per geometric stratum of Table~\ref{tab:geom-strata}, again $600$ positive and $600$ negative, with the photometric block disabled and only the stratum's geometric transformation applied to the second branch of each positive pair, so that per-stratum $\mathrm{Recall}$ isolates the model's response to that single transformation. No augmentation hyperparameter, no decision threshold and no class weight is changed between training and either evaluation regime.

\paragraph{Stratification by geometric transformation.} Per-transformation $\mathrm{FPR}$ and $\mathrm{Recall}$ are reported on the geometric strata listed in Table~\ref{tab:geom-strata}, chosen to separate exact $D_4$-equivariance from continuous $SE(2)$-equivariance. The strata are: the identity, the three discrete rotations of $D_4$, the two reflections of $D_4$, two off-$D_4$ rotations that lie in $SE(2)$ but not in $D_4$, and a translation. The identity stratum measures performance under photometric perturbation only and serves as a control; the discrete-rotation and flip strata test $D_4$-coverage; the off-$D_4$ rotations probe continuous-angle behaviour; the translation stratum tests the second factor of $SE(2)$.

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:per-stratum} reports, for each of the five trained models, the overall $\mathrm{FPR}$ and $\mathrm{Recall}$ on the $1\,200$-pair test with training-time augmentation, and the per-stratum $\mathrm{Recall}$ on each geometric stratum of Table~\ref{tab:geom-strata}, all at the fixed threshold $\tau = 0.5$. Rows are grouped into a common photometric-only baseline and the two per-$G$ comparisons. The photometric-only ViT-Tiny baseline behaves as a control: overall $\mathrm{Recall}$ is $99.83\%$ because the overall regime applies only the photometric block to its second branch, but per-stratum $\mathrm{Recall}$ drops to $11$--$31\%$ on the $D_4$ orbit and collapses to $\le 1.2\%$ on $R_{45}$, $R_{135}$ and $T_{0.2}$, which confirms that the encoder by itself carries no geometric robustness. In the $D_4$ comparison the augmented ViT-Tiny baseline and the Octic ViT both reach $100\%$ Recall on every $D_4$ stratum, so the architectural $D_4$-invariance fully replaces the corresponding geometric augmentation block; and at the same time the overall $\mathrm{FPR}$ of the Octic ViT is $1.17\%$ versus $3.67\%$ for the augmented ViT-Tiny, a $2.5$ percentage-point improvement at matched $\mathrm{Recall}$, so the architectural inductive bias does not only match augmentation on the orbit, it more than triples the precision of the augmented baseline at fixed threshold. Both models still fail on the off-orbit strata $R_{45}$, $R_{135}$ and $T_{0.2}$ ($\le 1\%$ Recall), as expected for an encoder whose invariance is restricted to $D_4$. In the $SE(2)$ comparison the augmented ViT-Tiny reaches $100\%$ on the $D_4$ strata and $99.17\%$ on $T_{0.2}$ but only $31.00\%$ and $30.67\%$ on $R_{45}$ and $R_{135}$, with the highest overall $\mathrm{FPR}$ in the table ($16.17\%$) and overall $\mathrm{Recall}$ that drops to $95.83\%$, indicating that the continuous-rotation augmentation distribution under-samples off-orbit angles and adds noise to the negative side. The Harmformer trained without geometric augmentation reaches $100\%$ on the discrete $D_4$ rotations and $94.17\%$ on the two flips, but $\le 0.67\%$ on $R_{45}/R_{135}$ and $2.50\%$ on $T_{0.2}$, with overall $\mathrm{FPR}$ of $12.00\%$ and overall $\mathrm{Recall}$ of $99.83\%$.

\paragraph{Discussion.} The two comparisons return different verdicts on the equivariance hypothesis $H_1$. For $G = D_4$, $H_1$ is supported in both directions: the architecturally $D_4$-invariant Octic ViT matches the augmented baseline on Recall along every $D_4$ stratum and improves $\mathrm{FPR}$ by $2.5$ percentage points (and overall precision from $1/(1+0.0367)$ to $1/(1+0.0117)$), so the geometric augmentation block can be dropped without loss of detection performance, exactly the experimental claim of Section~\ref{sec:aug}. For $G = SE(2)$, the verdict is mixed: the Harmformer beats the augmented ViT-Tiny on overall $\mathrm{FPR}$ ($12.00\%$ vs $16.17\%$) and on overall $\mathrm{Recall}$ ($99.83\%$ vs $95.83\%$) and reproduces the discrete $D_4$ orbit at $100\%$, yet collapses to under $1\%$ on the off-$D_4$ angles $R_{45}$ and $R_{135}$ and to $2.5\%$ on translation $T_{0.2}$, which are exactly the strata its architecture is supposed to cover, while the augmented ViT-Tiny reaches $31\%$ on $R_{45}/R_{135}$ and $99\%$ on $T_{0.2}$. The architectural advantage on overall metrics therefore does not come from continuous $SE(2)$-equivariance but from the absence of the noisy continuous-rotation augmentation that drags the $\mathrm{ViT-Tiny}+G_{SE(2)}$ baseline towards the decision threshold. The most likely cause of the off-$D_4$ collapse of the Harmformer is the small input resolution: the released code resizes images to $32\times 32$ before the encoder, and the circular harmonic filters rely on continuous rotation acting on a sufficiently sampled pixel grid; at $32\times 32$, bilinear interpolation around non-axis-aligned angles introduces enough discretization noise to break the analytic $SE(2)$-equivariance, which is consistent with $R_{45}/R_{135}$ Recall collapsing while the discrete $D_4$ orbit, where rotation acts by axis-aligned permutations of pixels, is unaffected. The augmented ViT-Tiny shows the symmetric failure on the same strata ($31\%$ Recall on $R_{45}/R_{135}$ despite continuous-angle training), which suggests that the augmentation distribution at $32\times 32$ is also not dense enough to cover $SO(2)$ effectively. Both observations point to the input resolution rather than to the equivariance principle as the binding constraint for the $SE(2)$ comparison; the $D_4$ result, by contrast, is robust to this issue because $D_4$ incurs no interpolation cost, which is the regime in which architectural $G$-invariance dominates.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:concl}

We compared architectural group invariance against geometric augmentation in a single siamese plagiarism detector, on the dihedral group of the square and the special Euclidean group of the plane. For the dihedral group the architecturally invariant encoder matches the augmented baseline on every right-angle rotation and reflection and improves precision on negatives, which supports the equivariance hypothesis and lets the geometric augmentation block be dropped without loss. For the special Euclidean group the verdict is mixed: the continuously equivariant encoder improves overall metrics and is exact on the discrete dihedral orbit, but fails on off-axis rotations and on translation, which we attribute to interpolation noise at low input resolution rather than to the equivariance principle. Future work will rerun the comparison at higher input resolution, so that arbitrary rotation angles act on enough pixels for the architectural invariance of the equivariant encoders to hold in practice, and broaden the set of tested transformations beyond rotations, reflections and translation to also cover scale and projective perturbations.
